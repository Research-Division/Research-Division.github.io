Imposed Tariffs & Effects: (CHN)

Overview

The Tariff Price Tool estimates how your imposed tariffs might affect consumer prices, measured through the personal consumption expenditures (PCE) index. A positive effect indicates a potential increase in consumer prices resulting from the tariffs. These effects are broken into two categories: direct and indirect effects.

Figure 1 shows the impact of your new tariffs and the original tariffs across the 21 Harmonized System sectors. Figure 2 breaks down the potential price effects across different consumer product categories, allowing you to explore how tariff costs might flow through the economy to affect household spending.

New vs. Original Tariffs by Section

This chart shows a comparison between the original tariff rates and the current imposed tariff rates by HS section. Missing bars indicate no tariff or no import data for that section.

Key observation: The largest tariffs are not necessarily the most likely to experience the most significant potential price effects. The impact depends on the import share of various consumption categories, their respective spending weight in the personal consumption expenditures (PCE) basket, the amount of cost increase from higher tariffs that firms choose to pass through, and the markup assumptions of intermediate input producing firms.

Note: The Tariff Price Tool Methodology Guide and FAQs provides an in-depth explanation of the Tariff Price Tool, its sources, assumptions, and calculations.

Price Effects by Category

Direct effects occur when retailers pass higher tariff costs directly to consumers by raising prices on imported goods. The size of this effect depends on how much of each product category is imported, how important it is in household spending, and how much of the tariff increase retailers choose to pass through to consumers.

Indirect effects capture how tariff costs spread through the economy via supply chains. When businesses use tariffed imports as production inputs, these higher costs can flow through to other products and services. This tool assumes firms pass these increased costs on to their customers.

Total effects represent the sum of direct and indirect effects.

This treemap visualization uses National Income and Products Account (NIPA) categorization to show how tariff effects are distributed across consumer product categories. Click on any category to explore its components. Each category shows both its percentage share of the total effect and the estimated annual dollar impact for the selected household income level.
